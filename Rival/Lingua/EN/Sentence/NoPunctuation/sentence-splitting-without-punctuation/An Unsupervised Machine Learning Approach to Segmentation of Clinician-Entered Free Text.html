An Unsupervised Machine Learning Approach to Segmentation of Clinician-Entered Free Text

Jesse O. Wrenn, BA,1 Peter D. Stetson, MD, MA,1,2 and Stephen B. Johnson, PhD1
1Department of Biomedical Informatics, Columbia University, New York NY
2Department of Medicine, Columbia University, New York, NY

Abstract

Natural language processing is an important tool in biomedicine, and fails without successful segmentation of words and sentences. Tokenization is a form of segmentation that identifies boundaries separating semantic units, for example words, dates, numbers and symbols, within a text. We sought to construct a highly generalizeable tokenization algorithm with no prior knowledge of characters or their function, based solely on the inherent statistical properties of token and sentence boundaries. Tokenizing clinician-entered free text, we achieved precision and recall of 92% and 93%, respectively compared to a whitespace token boundary detection algorithm. We classified over 80% of punctuation characters correctly, based on manual disambiguation with high inter-rater agreement (kappa = 0.916). Our algorithm effectively discovered properties of whitespace and punctuation in the corpus without prior knowledge of either. Given the dynamic nature of biomedical language, and the variety of distinct sublanguages used, the effectiveness and generalizability of our novel tokenization algorithm make it a valuable tool.

Introduction

Natural Language Processing (NLP) has many applications in biomedicine1,2. Medical performance measurement is on the rise3, and data about patient care, important in the assessment of quality4, may be extracted from textual records. Online literature is another important resource in biomedicine2, although there is now such a large quantity of literature that large-scale manual knowledge retrieval is impractical5.

NLP fails without successful segmentation, the process of separating text into processable units, such as words and sentences6. The high granularity of language allows for segmentation on different levels. The most fine-grained semantic units of language are morphemes. Tokens are semantically independent combinations of morphemes, and sentences are combinations of tokens. Tokenization is the process of finding boundaries between tokens in a text7.

Intuitively, we could consider punctuation and whitespace as indications of token boundaries. However, the ambiguity of punctuation8, especially in the medical language (?rales, d/c, –>, +/–, 0.05), makes it difficult to use it to draw conclusions. Furthermore, while whitespace may frequently exist between tokens, some semantic units consist of two or more words (diabetes mellitus, vital signs, S. aureus), and some boundaries are not indicated by whitespace (chest pain/palpitations/sob/diaphoresis/ nausea/vomiting). Inference of token boundaries using whitespace alone is therefore frequently inaccurate.

Most tokenizers are iteratively handcrafted on specific corpora. While such tokenizers may provide high corpus-specific recall and precision, they are very labor intensive to build and maintain, and rarely generalize well. Medicine has been shown to contain many distinct sublanguages with special properties9. Text that has been entered directly by clinicians is highly and inconsistently abbreviated10, arbitrarily capitalized and subject to dynamic changes. Therefore, the ability to quickly retrain a tokenization engine is integral to its long-term success. Examples of typing errors and arbitrarily punctuated, capitalized and abbreviated text segments from our set of clinician-entered text are as follows:

1) stll c sob when walks > 1 b;lock tring do excrecises […] continue susing flovent an datrovent as rxed and trying do do inc execrcise [sic]

[…] R int. carotid art., eval. by neurosurg. for ? R CEA but carotid dopplers c/w LICA <35% [sic]

Sentence boundary detection is also a complex problem. Clinician-entered free text frequently lacks sentence ending punctuation, and when it exists, it is often ambiguous7,8. Abbreviations, acronyms and contractions are very prominent in medicine10, adding to the difficulty of punctuation disambiguation. Question marks end interrogative sentences, but also frequently precede questionable findings in medical text.

We focused on generalizability and ease of maintenance as the most important features of a successful tokenization algorithm. Therefore we sought to construct an algorithm based on the inherent statistical properties of token boundaries. We hypothesized that changes in transitional entropy and conditional probability would effectively detect token boundaries, functionally disambiguating punctuation as well.

We evaluated our algorithm based on two problems: identification of boundaries between words and disambiguation of punctuation via tokenization. To evaluate the former, we compared our algorithm to a whitespace tokenizer. Although whitespace tokenization does not provide us with a perfect gold standard, it is very effective at finding boundaries between words, breaking only on multi-word tokens. We evaluated our algorithm’s ability to separate punctuation from words using a manually created gold standard.

Background

Zellig Harris11 first developed and established the utility of segmentation based on character entropy in 1967, although he used it to analyze words for their component morphemes. Using an English dictionary, Harris established the conditional probabilities of all occurring transitions between characters. He then established both the number of probable next neighbors and the reduction of entropy given each consecutive character, judging the latter to more accurately predict morpheme boundaries.

In 2001, John Goldsmith12 built on Harris’s work, choosing to use Minimum Description Length for the unsupervised acquisition of morphology of a natural language. He was able to achieve a precision of 85.9% and recall of 90.4% on a set of 1,000 English words.

More recently, Shamim Ara Mollah and Stephen Johnson13 reported on the automated learning of the morphology of a medical language. After initially using Harris’s algorithm, producing a set of probable morphemes, they pruned these morphemes based on the Mutual Information (MI) principle of Information Theory. On a set of 1,000 manually annotated medical words (generally far more complex than general English), they were able to achieve greater than 80% sensitivity and 90% specificity.

We expand on the aforementioned algorithms by applying them at a higher level, to the detection of token boundaries within texts, as opposed to the detection of morpheme boundaries within words.

Methods

We gathered a training set of 15,082 clinician-entered free text notes written over a three year period on WebCIS, a clinical information system used in both inpatient and outpatient settings at New York Presbyterian Hospital. Notes were, on average, approximately 160 words long and were of a variety of types, such as inpatient admission, transfer and progress notes and outpatient notes.

We then trained two tries (Figure 1) of arbitrarily large depth on each corpus, one reading forward and one reading backward. A trie is an ordered tree used to store associative arrays with strings as keys. The root node is associated with the empty string, and each edge is associated with a concatenated character. All descendants of the root represent a unique substring of the corpus and contain its total number of observed occurrences. We chose the trie data structure given its simplicity and its capacity to hold the number of occurrences of any substring of length less than or equal to the trie’s depth.

Trie data structure. The probability of observing an ‘s’ given the preceding string “Pt” is ¼, or 25%. The freedom following “pt” is 3.

To calculate conditional probability of observing a character given a preceding string, we divided the observed occurrences of the concatenation of the string and the character by the observed occurrences of the string by itself. We define freedom at character transitions as the size of the set of characters that were observed to follow a substring of the corpus. To calculate freedom, we counted the children of the trie node representing the string (Figure 1).

We analyzed text for specific statistical properties indicative of token boundaries. First, we calculated the forward- and backward-reading freedom of transitions following each substring of the text, lengths one through seven. We chose not to calculate the freedom substrings with a length of greater than seven because it was clear from previous work that the algorithm performed optimally using strings of length three through five, as is discussed later in the paper. We then used these values to assign additional statistical properties to characters, including increase and decrease in freedom. We assigned a “peak” value to each character transition, computed by adding the value of the preceding increase in freedom to the following decrease in freedom. We characterized token boundaries based on the sum of their forward- and backward-reading peak values.

To maximize effectiveness of the algorithm, we determined optimal values for substring length and the threshold above which summed peak values would indicate token boundaries. First, we randomly selected a test set of twenty clinician-entered free text notes. We then tokenized the notes in our test set using different combinations of these two values. We compared our results to those of a simple whitespace tokenizer, which identifies a boundary at every space, tab or line breaking character. We defined true positives as character transitions that were classified as token boundaries in both sets of results. False positives included instances of boundary detection by the freedom-based algorithm that were not identified by the whitespace tokenizer. True negatives included all transitions not identified by either method, and false negatives included instances of boundary detection by the whitespace algorithm that were not identified by the freedom-based algorithm.

For substring lengths three through six, we calculated average precision, recall (sensitivity), specificity and accuracy over a variable threshold. We graphed precision versus recall (PR), as well as sensitivity versus 1-specificity (ROC). We calculated areas under each curve, and used these statistics to compare effectiveness of different trie depths. Average precision, recall, specificity and accuracy served to compare effectiveness of threshold values, and the tradeoffs associated with each.

Having identified effective trie depth and threshold values, we used these values to tokenize the test set again. We then read through each automatically identified token individually, assigning probabilities to each character given all preceding characters in the token. To break sentence ending punctuation from the end of tokens, we identified new boundaries preceding characters that had a very low probability of occurrence within these tokens.

We asked two independent, outside raters to analyze sentence-ending punctuation from our test set of twenty notes. They were asked to mark each punctuation character as an independent token or as part of a larger token. Inter-rater reliability was measured using Cohen’s kappa. All disagreements were subsequently resolved by discussion. We then compared our algorithm’s results to the standard developed by our raters.

Results

Using substrings of length 3 to calculate conditional probability and freedom at character transitions, we were able to achieve a sensitivity of 99% (specificity 92%) with the threshold above which summed peak values would identify token boundaries set to 15. We achieved specificity of 99% (sensitivity 72%) setting the threshold to 40. Using substrings of length 4, we achieved sensitivity of 99% (specificity 93%) with a threshold of 25, and sensitivity of 86% (specificity 99%) with a threshold of 50. With substrings of length 5, we achieved sensitivity of 98% (specificity 94%) with a threshold of 20, and sensitivity of 88% (specificity 99%) with a threshold of 45. Using substrings of length 6, we achieved sensitivity of 98% (specificity 91%) with a threshold of 10, and sensitivity of 87% (specificity 99%) with a threshold of 35.

We obtained the area under the ROC curve (AUC) for substrings of length three to six (Table 1). The largest AUC was 0.990, achieved using substrings of length three. Substrings of length four, five and six achieved AUC of 0.988, 0.987 and 0.985, respectively.

We then obtained the area under each PR curve (AUPRC) (Table 1). The largest AUPRC, 0.974, was achieved using substrings of length four. Substrings of length five, six and three achieved AUPRC of 0.970, 0.966 and 0.945, respectively.

When asked to classify punctuation characters as either independent or part of a larger token, our raters found 468 punctuation characters. They agreed on the classification of 452 out of 468, or 96.6% (kappa = .916). Twelve of the sixteen disagreements involved classifications of question marks that preceded dependent words or phrases. The raters classified 139, or 29.7% of all punctuation characters as part of a larger token, and the remaining 329, or 70.3% as independent. Our algorithm correctly classified 106 out of 139, or 76% of dependent punctuation characters and 271 out of 329, or 82% of independent punctuation for a total of 81% of punctuation characters classified correctly with 89.1% precision 82.0% recall.

Discussion

Comparison between our algorithm and a simple whitespace tokenizer allowed us to determine the optimal substring length for tokenization. This is integral to the algorithm, as it must recognize common statistical patterns while simultaneously maximizing the length of patterns and their probability of occurring in the corpus. Although the tokenizer performed well with substrings of lower length in terms of AUC, precision is more important to tokenization than is specificity. Tokenizers must minimize incorrect boundary identifications, and precision measures this more accurately. Therefore, we gave more weight to the AUPRC, a reflection of precision and recall. When we used substrings of length four, the tokenizer clearly demonstrated higher performance as compared to when we used substrings of different lengths.

Also, high levels of agreement between our own algorithm and the whitespace tokenization algorithm suggested that our algorithm discovered the function of whitespace in the corpus. This is surprising, as our algorithm was not given any knowledge of the difference between whitespace characters and any other character.

When charged with identification of sentence-ending punctuation, our algorithm achieved precision and recall of 89.1% and 82.0%, respectively. Such performance without any knowledge of the function of punctuation around digits or the case of preceding and subsequent letters is also notable.

Rule-based tokenizers may be handcrafted to achieve arbitrarily high effectiveness. However, an important shortcoming of rule-based tokenizers is that they are time consuming to create and maintain. Each rule must be discovered, written and tested iteratively, and rules must not conflict with each other. Furthermore, any change in the corpus, such as the use of new tokens, may require correction of existing rules or creation of new rules to tokenize properly. With an unsupervised approach, such maintenance can be fully automated.

Supervised machine learning approaches require a set of manually tokenized documents on which to train. Expansion to a new distinct corpus involves the generation of a new training set, sacrificing portability. Although unsupervised machine learning techniques exist, they have been based on assumptions that characters such as whitespace, punctuation, digits and upper and lowercase letters fall into separate classes14. Our technique makes no such assumptions, and is therefore easily applied to corpora that regularly violate these assumptions.

The most important limitation of our work was the lack of gold standard against which we could evaluate our tokenization algorithm. Historically, tokenization is not well defined on purely linguistic or formal grounds, and success is dependent mostly on the underlying application15. The creation of a gold standard is very costly in terms of time and effort, and a single gold standard will not serve to effectively evaluate tokenization that is to be used for different purposes, and in different NLP systems.

Future studies should include comparison with a broad, manually generated gold standard. Such a gold standard should contain multi-word phrases judged to be semantically linked, justifying corresponding tokenization. Future studies should also include evaluation our tokenization algorithm with respect to its utility within a statistical NLP system. The characteristics and potential contribution of each of the many features assigned to character transitions by our algorithm may also contribute to our approach. There exists a deep relationship between tokenization and data compression in information theory that should also be studied in an effort to define tokenization in terms of its statistical properties.

Conclusions

The dynamic nature of biomedical language and the existence of many distinct sub-languages necessitate a general approach to tokenization. Our algorithm, effective at token boundary detection and punctuation disambiguation, is easily trained, offering excellent portability between corpora and adaptability to new user behavior.
